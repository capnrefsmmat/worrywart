The Worrywart

What should you worry about?

by Alex Reinhart

Most people worry about the wrong things. We are paranoid about terrorism despite it being millions of times more likely we will die in house fires or car accidents. We fear strangers in dark alleys when we're more likely to be attacked by spouses or friends.

The table below presents your annual risk of having various terrible things happen to you, expressed in units of micromorts: a one-in-a-million chance. So a 10 micromort probability represents a 10-in-a-million chance. Click or drag on the highlighted text—you can change all the variables to more accurately represent you.

All data is based on United States mortality and crime data, and may not be accurate for other countries. These are averages only—your personal risk depends on many factors, like your health, where you live, your job, and any risky behavior you might enjoy.

Sources

All data was collected from academic studies or government sources, and reflects the best estimates I could find. If any of these sources are out of date or inaccurate, please contact me to let me know.

Disease mortality data
Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, "Health, United States, 2014".
Transportation death rates
Ian Savage, "Comparing the fatality risks in United States transportation across modes and over time", Research in Transportation Economics (2013), vol 43, p. 9–22.
National Highway Traffic Safety Administration, "National Pedestrian Crash Report", 2008.
Crime rates
Bureau of Justice Statistics, National Crime Victimization Survey, 2014 data. The NCVS uses a representative sample of Americans and asks about their experiences of crime, rather than relying on crimes reported to police, making it more reliable for crimes that are often undercounted, like rape. Some figures are still unreliable because of small samples and insufficient data, and there may still be underreporting..
Fire deaths
Federal Emergency Management Agency, "Fire death and injury rates (2004-2013)"
